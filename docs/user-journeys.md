# User Journeys — ismybabyalright.com

Each journey maps: **Trigger → Search → Landing → Experience → Outcome → After**

These journeys describe what ACTUALLY happens today (not what we want to build).
They expose gaps, pain points, and opportunities.

---

## Journey 1: Nadia — The 2am Googler

### Trigger
It's 2:17am. She's breastfeeding. Her 4-month-old hasn't rolled over yet. A friend posted a video today of her same-age baby rolling both ways. The thought lands: "is that supposed to happen by now?"

### Search
She types one-handed into Google: "4 month old not rolling over." She gets:
- Healthline article (says 4-6 months — partially reassuring)
- BabyCenter forum from 2014 (useless)
- A scary article about motor delays
- A pediatric clinic blog that says "talk to your doctor"

She opens 4 tabs. Reads pieces of each. Gets conflicting timelines.

### Landing (hypothetical: ismybabyalright.com)
She finds the site in search results. The name catches her — it's literally her question.

### What she needs from the experience
- Enter "4 months" → immediately see rolling milestones with a RANGE, not a single number
- A clear answer: "Most babies roll between 3-6 months. At 4 months, not rolling yet is within the normal range."
- A "when to actually call your doctor" threshold so she can stop guessing
- Something she can read one-handed in under 60 seconds

### Ideal outcome
She exhales. She puts the phone down. She finishes the feeding. She sleeps. She doesn't open 6 more tabs.

### After
She bookmarks the site. Two weeks later, she comes back when she has a question about babbling. She tells her mom friend group about it.

## Journey 4: James — The Experienced Parent Who's Newly Worried

### Trigger
James is watching his 11-month-old son sit on the floor surrounded by toys. His son isn't pulling to stand. He isn't cruising. He isn't interested in finger foods. James pulls up his daughter's baby photos on his phone. At 11 months, she was standing at the coffee table. The gap is undeniable.

### Search
He types: "11 month old not pulling to stand." Then adds a second search: "is it normal for second child to be slower." He gets:
- Milestone charts that list "pulling to stand: 9-12 months" — his son is in range, technically
- A parenting article that says "boys can be slower" (feels like a cliché, not data)
- A developmental checklist that doesn't show the gradient between "a little behind" and "a lot behind"

### What he needs from the experience
- The full bell curve: not just "9-12 months" but the distribution — when do 10% of kids do it? 50%? 90%?
- A way to log what his son CAN do today so he can track progress month to month
- Clear criteria for when "wait and see" is appropriate vs. when to push for evaluation
- Acknowledgment that comparing to a sibling is normal AND not always useful

### Ideal outcome
He sees that his son is in the lower-but-normal range. He also sees a clear threshold: "If not pulling to stand by 12 months, discuss with your pediatrician." He has one month and a specific thing to watch for. The ambiguity shrinks.

### After
He checks back at 12 months. His son is pulling to stand by then and James exhales. But he keeps the site bookmarked because the tracking was useful.

### Trigger

### Search

### What she needs from the experience

- No jargon: "transfers objects" → "passes a toy from one hand to the other"

### Ideal outcome
She finally understands what the doctor is asking about. She feels informed, not tested. She can observe her baby with a framework she understands, not one she's guessing at.

### After
Before every well-child visit, she checks the site to understand what the doctor will be looking for. She stops feeling blindsided by screening forms. She starts asking questions at appointments instead of just nodding.

## Journey 22: Liam — The Autistic Dad

### Trigger
Liam watches his 14-month-old son spin the wheels of a toy car for 12 minutes straight. He knows that look. He used to do the same thing with ceiling fans. His wife says "he just really likes that car." Liam thinks: "It's not the car. It's the spinning."

### Search
He types: "autism signs 14 months." He gets:
- Deficit-based checklists: "Does not make eye contact," "Does not respond to name," "Does not point"
- Articles framing autism as a devastating diagnosis
- Parent stories of grief and mourning after a diagnosis
- Nothing written from the perspective of an autistic person evaluating their own child

### What he needs from the experience
- A screening tool that describes behaviors neutrally, not as deficits
- Information about what early intervention actually does — it's support, not "fixing"
- The practical pathway: how to request an evaluation, what the process looks like, what the timeline is
- Resources that are neurodiversity-affirming — that don't treat his own neurotype as a tragedy

### Ideal outcome
He finds a screening tool that confirms what he already suspects. The tone is respectful, not pitying. He learns how to request an evaluation through his state's early intervention program. He starts the process.

### After
His son is evaluated and qualifies for speech therapy and OT. Liam is the most prepared parent the therapists have ever worked with — because he understands from the inside. The site helped him navigate the system, not mourn a diagnosis.

## Journey 23: Natalie — Watching Her Baby Disappear

### Trigger
Natalie is looking at photos from last month. Her 20-month-old daughter is waving in one of them. She tries to get her daughter to wave now. Nothing. She says "mama." Her daughter doesn't look up. Two months ago, she would have. Natalie pulls out her phone and searches with trembling hands: "toddler losing words regression."

### Search
The results are terrifying:
- "Regression in toddlers" immediately links to autism, Rett syndrome, metabolic disorders
- She reads about autistic regression — loss of language between 15-24 months
- She finds a medical article about "disintegrative disorder" and can barely breathe
- Nothing tells her what to do RIGHT NOW — everything says "see your doctor" but the specialist wait is 6 weeks

### What she needs from the experience
- Immediate acknowledgment: "Loss of skills is different from slow development and should be evaluated promptly"
- Specific guidance: "Document what she could do, when she stopped, and bring this to your pediatrician. Use the word 'regression' — it triggers a different response than 'slow development.'"
- A checklist for documenting regression: which skills were present, when they were lost, how quickly
- Triage: "If regression is accompanied by [these symptoms], go to the ER. Otherwise, call your pediatrician and insist on an urgent referral."

### Ideal outcome
She has a documentation template. She records everything. She calls the pediatrician and says "my daughter is experiencing developmental regression" — the word "regression" gets her a faster referral. She is taken seriously.

### After
The evaluation leads to a diagnosis and an intervention plan. The documentation she started on the site becomes part of the medical record. She says: "That site gave me the language to be heard."

## Journey 24: Carmen — Baby With a Known Diagnosis

### Trigger
Carmen takes her 10-month-old daughter (who has Down syndrome) to a mommy-and-me music class. The instructor says "by 10 months, most babies are clapping!" Carmen's daughter isn't clapping. The other moms look at Carmen with that look — the pitying one. Carmen doesn't need pity. She needs the music teacher to understand that her daughter is working on clapping in OT and will get there on HER timeline.

### Search
She types: "Down syndrome milestones 10 months." She finds:
- The NDSS milestone chart — helpful but basic
- Generic milestone trackers that make her daughter look dramatically behind
- A few blog posts from DS parents — relatable but not systematic
- Nothing that lets her track her daughter's progress against DS-specific benchmarks interactively

### What she needs from the experience
- A milestone tracker where she can select her daughter's diagnosis and see ADJUSTED milestones
- Growth charts specific to Down syndrome (they exist — the AAP published them)
- A way to track and celebrate progress on HER child's timeline
- Something she can show other people — the music teacher, relatives, strangers — that reframes the conversation

### Ideal outcome
She tracks her daughter's milestones against DS-specific benchmarks. Her daughter is actually ON TRACK for a child with Down syndrome. Carmen has data to counter the pity and reframe the narrative.

### After
She uses the tracker at every therapy session and pediatrician visit. She shares it with other DS parents. She becomes an advocate for diagnosis-adjusted developmental tracking.

## Journey 25: Sasha — The Mom in a Wheelchair

### Trigger
Sasha reads a parenting article that says "tummy time is essential for motor development — get on the floor with your baby!" She can't easily get on and off the floor. She's been doing tummy time on her lap and on a raised mat on the couch. Her son is 5 months old and doing great — lifting his head, pushing up on his arms. But the article makes her wonder: is her adaptation enough? Is she limiting his development because of her body?

### Search
She types: "tummy time alternatives disability parent." She finds almost nothing.
- Every article assumes an able-bodied parent on the floor
- One occupational therapy blog mentions chest-to-chest tummy time — useful but incomplete
- Nothing addresses the broader question: how to support development when you can't do what the standard advice assumes

### What she needs from the experience
- Milestone information with ALTERNATIVE METHODS noted: "Tummy time can be done on the floor, on your chest, on a raised surface, or on your lap — all are effective"
- Acknowledgment that adaptive parenting IS parenting — she's not doing a lesser version
- Activity suggestions that don't assume specific physical abilities
- A way to confirm her son is on track that doesn't measure HER body, just HIS development

### Ideal outcome
She sees that her son's development is normal. She learns that her tummy time adaptations are evidence-based and effective. She stops doubting her body and starts trusting her son's progress.

### After
She writes a blog post about adaptive parenting tips and links to the resource. She becomes a vocal advocate for accessible parenting guidance.

## Journey 26: Derek — Still in the Delivery Room

### Trigger
Derek's 2-month-old daughter is napping. She's been asleep for 3 hours, which is normal, but Derek keeps checking on her. He touches her hand. She grips his finger reflexively. He exhales. He goes back to the living room. Five minutes later, he checks again. He whispers to himself: "She's fine. She's fine." He picks up his phone and types: "low APGAR score long term effects."

### Search
He finds:
- Medical studies about APGAR scores and developmental outcomes — dense and statistical
- A terrifying article about hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy (HIE)
- A parenting forum where someone describes the same birth experience and their child is fine at age 5
- Another forum where someone describes the same experience and their child has cerebral palsy
- He doesn't know which story is his.

### What he needs from the experience
- A clear, calm explanation of what APGAR scores mean and what they DON'T mean
- "A low 1-minute APGAR that improves by 5 minutes (like your daughter's) is generally not associated with long-term outcomes"
- A list of specific things to watch for — and specific things NOT to worry about
- Recognition that birth trauma in fathers is real and valid

### Ideal outcome
He reads the APGAR context and understands — for the first time — that his daughter's trajectory (3 → 8) is reassuring. He sees the signs to actually watch for. Most of them don't apply. He also sees a note: "If you experienced a frightening birth, you may be carrying trauma. It's okay to talk to someone." He considers it.

### After
The anxiety gradually decreases as his daughter hits milestones normally. At 6 months, he tells his wife about how scared he was. She had no idea. He talks to a therapist once. It helps more than any website.

## Journey 27: Jen — The Mom Whose Baby Won't Grow

### Trigger
Jen puts her 4-month-old on the kitchen scale. 11 lbs 6 oz. Yesterday it was 11 lbs 7 oz. She knows daily fluctuation is normal. She weighs him again in an hour. 11 lbs 5 oz. She writes all three numbers down. She calculates his daily caloric intake from the feeding log. She types: "4 month old failure to thrive weight gain."

### Search
She finds:
- Clinical definitions of failure to thrive — weight below 5th percentile or crossing two major percentile lines
- Breastfeeding websites saying "trust your body, your supply is enough" — she can't trust that anymore
- Formula feeding guides — she's already supplementing
- A medical article listing causes of FTT: undereating, malabsorption, metabolic disorders, cardiac issues — she spirals through each one

### What she needs from the experience
- A growth percentile calculator where she can input weight, length, head circumference, and age — and see her son's actual position on the WHO curve
- The ability to plot multiple data points over time to see the TRAJECTORY, not just the snapshot
- Clear thresholds: "Concern increases when a baby drops across two major percentile lines over X time period"
- Guidance on what questions to ask the pediatrician and what tests to request

### Ideal outcome
She plots her son's growth. She sees the trajectory clearly. She brings the chart to the pediatrician and says: "Here's what I'm seeing." The doctor orders tests. They find a posterior tongue tie affecting feeding efficiency. It gets corrected. Weight gain improves.

### After
She tracks his growth weekly, then monthly. The curve starts climbing. She stops weighing him on the kitchen scale. She keeps the tool for the growth chart feature.

## Journey 28: Raj — Can't Stop Looking at His Baby's Head

### Trigger
Raj takes the weekly overhead photo of his 4-month-old son's head. He compares it to last week's. The flat spot is the same. Maybe worse. He puts his phone down, picks it up, and looks again. He measures the head circumference: 41.5cm. Last week: 41.2cm. He opens a browser.

### Search
He types: "flat head 4 months getting worse." He finds:
- A pediatric neurosurgery site explaining craniosynostosis (terrifying)
- An article about positional plagiocephaly (probably this, but how can he be sure?)
- Helmet therapy company websites that feel like sales pitches
- His pediatrician's voice echoing: "it'll round out"

### What he needs from the experience
- A visual guide: what does positional plagiocephaly look like vs. craniosynostosis? With images.
- A decision matrix: "If the flat spot is [characteristics], it's likely positional. If [other characteristics], request a specialist evaluation."
- Information about the helmet therapy window: when it works, when it's too late, what the evidence actually says
- A way to track head shape progression with measurements over time

### Ideal outcome
He identifies his son's presentation as consistent with positional plagiocephaly (not craniosynostosis). He understands the helmet window is 4-8 months and decides to push for a specialist referral now rather than waiting. He gets the appointment.

### After
The specialist confirms positional plagiocephaly. They decide on helmet therapy. Raj tracks the improvement on the site. At 10 months, the asymmetry has improved significantly.

## Journey 29: Vanessa — The Mom Whose Doctor Didn't Listen

### Trigger
Vanessa leaves the 12-month appointment frustrated. She told the pediatrician — again — that her son has low muscle tone, isn't pulling to stand, and feels floppy when she holds him. The doctor — again — said "some kids are late walkers, give it time." She sits in her car and thinks: "If I were a white mom saying this, would he be referring me to a neurologist right now?"

### Search
She types: "how to get pediatrician to take concerns seriously." Then: "can I self-refer for early intervention." Then: "12 month old low muscle tone hypotonia."
- She finds information about her state's early intervention program — parents CAN self-refer
- She reads about racial disparities in pediatric diagnoses — Black children are diagnosed with developmental delays later, on average, than white children
- She finds a checklist for hypotonia that matches her son closely
- She discovers she doesn't need her pediatrician's permission to start the process

### What she needs from the experience
- A developmental screening she can complete and document — so her observations become DATA, not just "a concerned mom"
- Information about self-referral for early intervention in her state
- A printable summary she can bring to the doctor — or to a new doctor — that's harder to dismiss
- A list of specific phrases to use: "I would like a referral to a pediatric neurologist. If you're declining, please document the refusal in the chart."

### Ideal outcome
She self-refers to early intervention. A therapist evaluates her son at home and confirms low muscle tone. With that documentation, she goes back to the pediatrician — or a new one — with professional validation. She is finally heard.

### After
Her son starts physical therapy. He starts pulling to stand at 15 months. Vanessa becomes an advocate in her community, teaching other Black mothers about self-referral. She shares the tool that helped her build her case.

## Journey 30: Maria — The Mom Who Can't Read the Forms

### Trigger
Maria takes her 9-month-old to the WIC office. They give her a handout about developmental milestones. It's in English. She recognizes some words but not others. "Transfers objects" — what does that mean? "Responds to own name" — her daughter does this in Spanish, does that count? She folds the handout into her purse. At home, she unfolds it and stares at it.

### Search
She asks her niece to help her search: "desarrollo del bebé 9 meses" (baby development 9 months). She finds:
- A Spanish-language parenting site with good info but for a Latin American medical context
- Google-translated versions of American milestone charts that sound awkward
- YouTube videos in Spanish about baby development — helpful but hard to match to the American screening framework
- Nothing that bridges her language with her medical system

### What she needs from the experience
- A milestone guide in natural, conversational Spanish — not Google-translated
- Visual/video content that shows what each milestone looks like (transcends language)
- Bilingual output she can bring to the doctor: "My daughter can do these things (in Spanish and English)"
- Acknowledgment that milestones look the same across languages — a baby responding to her name in Spanish counts

### Ideal outcome
She goes through a visual milestone guide she actually understands. She can show the doctor a clear, bilingual summary. The doctor sees what her daughter CAN do, not what Maria couldn't communicate. For the first time, she feels like a full participant in her daughter's healthcare, not a bystander.

### After
She tells other moms at church about the resource. It spreads through her community. The WIC office starts recommending it to Spanish-speaking families.
